KIIP Level 5 — Understanding Korean Society

A self-study reference for 한국사회 이해 (기본·심화) · Korea Immigration & Integration Program

Note on this level

KIIP Level 5 is not a Korean-language grammar course. It is the 한국사회 이해 (Understanding Korean Society) stage — a basic course (기본, 70 hours) and an advanced course (심화, 30 hours) covering Korea's geography, history, government, law, economy, society, and culture. Passing its evaluation can substitute for parts of the naturalization test. This reference is therefore a thematic study guide of key facts and terms, organized by subject area — not grammar lessons. It summarizes the topics in plain English with the essential Korean vocabulary you will meet.

The chapters below group the core content of the Understanding Korean Society course into eight themes. Each gives a short explanation of what you should know, followed by a table of key terms (핵심 용어) in Korean and English. Use it to review for the 종합평가 (comprehensive evaluation) and to build the civic vocabulary that the permanent-residency and naturalization tracks expect.

THEME 01지리Geography & Regions

설명 What to know

The Republic of Korea (대한민국) occupies the southern half of the Korean Peninsula in Northeast Asia, bordered by the sea on three sides and by North Korea across the Demilitarized Zone (DMZ). About 70% of the land is mountainous, so most people live in the plains and river valleys, where large cities have grown. The country is highly urbanized — roughly half the population lives in the Seoul Capital Area (수도권).

  • Capital: Seoul (서울), on the Han River (한강).
  • Administrative divisions: special/metropolitan cities (특별시·광역시) and provinces (도). Examples: 경기도, 강원특별자치도, 충청도, 전라도, 경상도, 제주특별자치도.
  • Climate: four distinct seasons (사계절) — spring, summer (with a rainy season 장마), autumn, and a cold, dry winter.
  • Major cities: Seoul, Busan (부산, the largest port), Incheon (인천, the main airport), Daegu, Daejeon, Gwangju, Ulsan.

Key fact South Korea's population is about 51 million, but the birth rate is among the world's lowest and the society is aging rapidly (고령화).

THEME 02역사History

설명 What to know

Korea has a long recorded history. According to legend, the first state, Gojoseon (고조선), was founded by Dangun (단군). The peninsula was later organized into the Three Kingdoms (삼국 시대) — Goguryeo, Baekje, and Silla — which Silla unified. The Goryeo (고려) dynasty, from which the name "Korea" comes, was followed by the long Joseon (조선) dynasty, founded in 1392, when King Sejong (세종대왕) created the Korean alphabet, Hangul (한글), in the 15th century.

  • Japanese colonial period (일제 강점기), 1910–1945: Korea lost its sovereignty; the March 1st Independence Movement (3·1 운동) of 1919 was a key resistance.
  • Liberation (광복), 1945, then division of the peninsula; the Republic of Korea was established in 1948.
  • Korean War (한국 전쟁), 1950–1953, ended in an armistice, leaving the country divided at the DMZ.
  • Rapid development: from the 1960s, swift industrialization (the "Miracle on the Han River" 한강의 기적) and the democratization movement of the 1980s led to today's democratic, advanced economy.

Key fact Hangul Day (한글날, Oct 9) celebrates the creation of the alphabet; Liberation Day (광복절, Aug 15) marks the end of colonial rule.

THEME 03정치Government & Democracy

설명 What to know

South Korea is a democratic republic (민주공화국) whose sovereignty rests with the people (국민). Power is separated into three branches (삼권 분립): the legislature, the executive, and the judiciary. The Constitution (헌법) is the highest law and guarantees citizens' basic rights.

  • Executive (행정부): headed by the President (대통령), the head of state, elected directly to a single five-year term.
  • Legislature (입법부): the National Assembly (국회), whose members (국회의원) are elected to make laws.
  • Judiciary (사법부): the courts (법원), which interpret and apply the law independently; the Constitutional Court (헌법재판소) reviews constitutional questions.
  • Elections (선거): citizens 18 and older may vote. Korea holds presidential, National Assembly, and local elections by the principles of universal, equal, direct, and secret voting.

Key fact The three-branch separation of powers (삼권 분립) exists so that no single branch becomes too powerful — a core principle of Korean democracy.

THEME 04법Law & Rights

설명 What to know

The Constitution guarantees every person fundamental rights (기본권) and also sets out duties (의무). Laws apply equally to everyone, and disputes are settled in court rather than privately. For immigrants, understanding everyday law — labor, family, residence, and reporting procedures — is an important part of this level.

  • Basic rights (기본권): human dignity, equality (평등), freedom (자유), the right to participate in politics (참정권), and social rights such as education and welfare.
  • Basic duties (의무): paying taxes (납세), education (교육), work (근로), national defense (국방), and protecting the environment.
  • Everyday law: labor law protects wages and working hours; family law covers marriage, divorce, and child custody; foreigners must keep their residence registration (외국인 등록) current.
  • Help & reporting: emergencies — police 112, fire/ambulance 119; legal help is available through public legal-aid services.

Key fact Rights and duties go together: the Constitution protects your freedoms while also asking every member of society to fulfill basic obligations such as paying taxes.

THEME 05경제Economy & Work

설명 What to know

Korea built one of the world's advanced economies in a few decades, moving from agriculture to manufacturing and then to high technology. It is a market economy (시장 경제) driven by exports (수출) — semiconductors, cars, ships, and culture (K-pop, film). The currency is the won (원).

  • Industry: large conglomerates (대기업) and many small and medium-sized firms (중소기업) coexist; Korea is a major exporter of electronics and automobiles.
  • Work life: a standard labor contract (근로 계약) sets wages and hours; the minimum wage (최저임금) is set each year; the four major insurances (4대 보험) cover health, pension, employment, and accident.
  • Finance: banks handle accounts, transfers, and loans; income is subject to tax (세금), and year-end tax settlement (연말정산) applies to employees.
  • Job-seeking: employment centers and online portals help with hiring; foreigners must hold a visa status that permits their type of work.

Key fact Most employees are enrolled in the 4대 보험 (national health insurance, national pension, employment insurance, industrial-accident insurance) — a core part of working life in Korea.

THEME 06사회Society & Welfare

설명 What to know

Korean society is changing fast: families are smaller, more people live alone, the population is aging, and Korea is becoming multicultural (다문화 사회) as more immigrants settle. The government runs a social-welfare (사회 복지) system and many support services for residents, including immigrant families.

  • Social safety net: the four insurances plus basic livelihood support for those in need; public health centers (보건소) offer low-cost services.
  • Family & care: childcare subsidies and facilities, support for the elderly (long-term care insurance 노인 장기요양보험), and disability services.
  • Multicultural support: Multicultural Family Support Centers (다문화가족지원센터) and the Social Integration Program (사회통합프로그램) help immigrants with Korean, counseling, and settlement.
  • Public services: the resident/foreigner registration system, the 정부24 online portal, and community service centers (행정복지센터) handle everyday paperwork.

Key fact 다문화가족지원센터 (Multicultural Family Support Centers) provide free Korean classes, interpretation, and family counseling for immigrants nationwide.

THEME 07문화Culture & Daily Life

설명 What to know

Korean culture blends long traditions with a dynamic modern life. Confucian values still shape respect for elders and politeness in language (높임말). Major traditional holidays are Seollal (설날, Lunar New Year) and Chuseok (추석, harvest festival), when families gather, perform ancestral rites, and eat special foods. Modern Korean culture — film, drama, and K-pop — is known worldwide as the Korean Wave (한류).

  • Holidays: 설날 (rice-cake soup 떡국, New Year bows 세배) and 추석 (songpyeon 송편, visiting ancestral graves 성묘).
  • Etiquette: using two hands when giving/receiving, bowing in greeting, removing shoes indoors, and using honorific speech to elders.
  • Food: rice, kimchi (김치), soups and stews, and shared side dishes (반찬); communal, seasonal eating is valued.
  • Traditional arts: hanbok (한복), hanok houses (한옥), and heritage like Hangul and royal palaces.

Key fact 설날 and 추석 are the two biggest holidays; on both, families gather, hold ancestral rites (차례), and travel to hometowns, causing the nation's largest movement of people.

THEME 08시민Citizenship & Integration

설명 What to know

The final theme connects everything to life as a member of Korean society. It covers the immigration and residence system, the path to permanent residency (영주) and naturalization (귀화), and the civic responsibilities of being part of a community — voting where eligible, paying taxes, respecting others, and participating in local life.

  • Status & documents: alien registration card (외국인 등록증), visa status (체류 자격), and the Social Integration Program (사회통합프로그램) as a route that benefits residence and naturalization applications.
  • Permanent residency & naturalization: 영주권 (permanent residency) and 귀화 (acquiring citizenship); passing the KIIP comprehensive evaluation (종합평가) can replace parts of the naturalization test.
  • Civic life: community participation, respecting diversity, and knowing where to get help (행정복지센터, 출입국·외국인청 — immigration office).
  • Being a good neighbor: following public rules, separating recycling, keeping public order, and contributing to the community.

Key fact Completing the Social Integration Program (사회통합프로그램) and passing the 종합평가 can exempt applicants from parts of the naturalization written test and interview.
